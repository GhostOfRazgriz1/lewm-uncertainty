A world model's uncertainty is a monitor, not a controller

Selective prediction on a frozen LeWorldModel (LeWM, arXiv:2603.19312) over Push-T. The constructive result (M1.6) of the uncertainty thread — no retraining.

A JEPA world model can't use its uncertainty to decide better (planning & active-sensing are null on two substrates). But it can use it to know when to abstain from its own prediction — and the two free signals it exposes are complementary, together recovering up to 91% of an oracle's selective-prediction skill.

How it works — architecture

The model & its two free signals

read off the frozen LeWM, no retraining

  • LeWM: ViT encoder → 192-d latent kept on a Gaussian shell by a SIGReg term; an action-conditioned predictor rolls the latent forward.
  • shell deviation = |∥emb∥ − shell| — an OOD / epistemic signal (“is this input familiar?”).
  • MC-dropout variance of the predictor — a predictive-error signal (“will I be wrong?”), Pearson +0.41 vs rollout error.
  • combined = z-score(MC) + z-score(shell) — both facets at once.

The monitor — selective prediction

turn a calibrated signal into an abstain decision

  • For each prediction, rank transitions by a signal; keep the most-confident fraction c (coverage), abstain on the rest.
  • Measure mean error on the kept set → a risk–coverage curve; summarise by AURC (area, lower = better).
  • References: oracle (rank by the true error — the floor) and random (flat at the mean error — no-skill ceiling).
  • Two failure modes, reported separately so the win isn't a trivial OOD-sort: in-distribution (hard transitions) and shift (corrupted inputs).

The shell: in-distribution vs OOD

distribution of ∥emb∥ — the encoder's latent norm

In-dist embeddings cluster on the shell (green dashed line, √192 ≈ 13.9) because SIGReg pushes them to N(0,I) and high-d Gaussians live on that sphere. OOD (noise-corrupted) inputs were never regularized, so the encoder maps them off the shell. The signal is the distance from the line — large only for OOD.

MC-dropout: predictions tight vs scattered

K dropout passes — their spread is the signal

Keeping dropout on and predicting K times samples an ensemble from the model itself: on an easy transition the K predictions agree (low variance), on a hard one they scatter (high variance). That spread (x) rises with true prediction error (y) — high variance ⇒ the model will be wrong.

How it's used at deploy

At inference the signal is a one-line read off the model. Pick an operating point on the risk–coverage curve (a threshold), then act when confident, abstain / flag / re-plan when the signal is high. Match the signal to the failure mode you expect: MC-variance for in-distribution predictive error, shell for distribution shift, combined when both are in play. Reproduce: python src/monitor.py.

How to read it

  • Shorter bar = better monitor. The green bar (oracle) is the best achievable; the gray dashed line (random) is no-skill. A signal is useful when its bar sits left of random and near oracle — the % gap recovered quantifies it.
  • Complementary facets. MC-variance wins in-distribution (recovers ~60% of the skill gap) where shell is useless (it's an OOD signal, and there's no OOD — orthogonal, as expected). On the mixed pool the shell catches the corruption and combined recovers ~91% — each facet covers a failure mode the other is blind to.
  • The honest caveat. Combining hurts in-distribution (the shell is pure noise there). The rule: use the facet that matches your failure mode; combine only when both are in play.
